TextMap

The app that stores information about the world around you , available by search or map - making real world objects and text accessible

Problem

We live in a world of information, but increasingly we find our access to information has been severed. Be it visual impairment, social anxiety, or the information simply not being accessible, a sizeable portion of the population has been cut off. TextMap is the response to these issues. Using the tools already available to us, smartphones and screen readers, TextMap uses GPS-technology combined with user-input to give you the information you can not see from the world around you. Helpful to the visually impaired and sighted people alike, TextMap allows you to know exactly what you need before entering any establishment. It also helps the visually impaired read signs they cannot reach, and any text they encounter abroad.

Survey

Visual impairment is defined as the degradation of eyesight to the point where common methods do not work. The World Health Organization released an article on visual impairment in 2010. The study concluded an estimate that around 285 million were affected by visual impairment. Out of those 285 million, a whopping 39 million are totally blind. Compared to the world population, this portion makes up a total of 4.25 percent. Visual impairment restricts several aspects of a persons life on a daily basis. Those who are impaired struggle especially social and occupational environments.


To scope out how relevant the issue is, we surveyed 42 respondants in an online survey published to a public forum dedicated to the Visually Impaired. After filtering the responses into those who confirmed they were Visually Impaired we found this:

Almost 89 percent of visually impaired respondents confirmed they encountered issues with text, information that was inaccessible to them. The remaining 11 percent answered that they rarely ever had issues. This was further confirmed by what else we found:

A majority of respondents reported that they did find active difficulties, day to day, with information that was not accessible to them due to their impairment. A whole 67.7 percent agreed with the statement, while a measely 5.9 percent were opposed to it. From this we understood the issue we were tackling was a relevant one for the visually impaired community.

Not only did those who were impaired interested, but some saw other uses to it than intended. Both charts showed that approximately 52 percent of each demographic saw use in the app. What is confirmed is that among our whole potential user base, the majority can see a real use in our software:

The survey had a box for user-feedback, where the respondents told us what they felt were missing from the software. For example, a respondent suggested we have a way to verify and rate the feedback, which we implemented into our software. We also had to find a way to report relocations and such, creating a network between us and various companies who used our software for accessibility.

Solution

TextMap is a software that builds on already existing solutions to help the visually impaired and people at large. Using the built-in GPS technology of smartphones and building on already existing screenreader applications for the visually impaired, we can extract information about establishments and signs, as they are put in by other users of the app. The app ships with a database that contains information about a large variety of establishments, but over time users would submit more and more information eventually creating a total oversight of restaurant menus, signs, entertainment information, etc. all verified and available in standard English.

1.

By default the application's menu will show you either a map of your surroundings or have it interpret it audibly for you. Based on the information in our database, the app will give you an oversight of locations that it supports. Users have also the ability to rate a document, to ensure trust in the application.

2.

From there on if you have selected a location, you will be presented with an array of information gathered from that place. A brief description of each element in the array can be given, to make the proccess more efficient. An option will also be given to not read up the information automatically, for those with moderate visual impairment.

3.

If there is information missing, users have the ability to submit their own to upload to the database. The picture will then be translated into text, and uploaded to the database. Different users can then view that information, as seen in the previous screenshot.

4.

The application gives you also the option display nearby locations or find a specific location, which you can find through the search engine. Users can also preview the information stored in those places.
